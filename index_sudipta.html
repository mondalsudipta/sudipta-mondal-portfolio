I am a PhD student in Computer Science at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County (UMBC), where I am a member of the Social Intelligence Lab. My research sits at the intersection of natural language processing and human-computer interaction, and I expect to graduate in 2030.

Prior to UMBC, I completed an M.Sc. and B.Sc. in Computer Science and Engineering at BRAC University (CGPA 3.88 and 3.49 respectively). During my master's I worked on self-supervised learning and published work on sarcasm detection in Bangla social media text.

My current research focuses on building AI systems that are interpretable, fair, and genuinely useful for people, with particular attention to how language models handle socially complex content such as hate speech, misinformation, and human emotion.

Interpretable Bangla Sarcasm Detection using BERT and Explainable AI
Ramisa Anan, Tasnim Sakib Apon, Zeba Tahsin Hossain, Elizabeth Antora Modhu, Sudipta Mondal, MD. Golam Rabiul Alam
2023 IEEE 13th Annual Computing and Communication Workshop and Conference (CCWC), Las Vegas, NV — March 2023
Published
A positive phrase or sentence with an underlying negative motive is defined as sarcasm, widely used on social media platforms such as Facebook, Twitter, and Reddit. This paper presents a BERT-based system achieving 99.60% accuracy for Bangla sarcasm detection, outperforming traditional ML approaches (89.93%). LIME is applied for interpretability, and the newly collected BanglaSarc dataset is introduced for evaluation.

This portfolio documents work completed for Human-Centered Computing coursework, demonstrating the application of interaction design principles, usability evaluation, and user-centered design methods.

Improving Usability of E-commerce and Industrial Websites using Interaction Design Principles
Duration: Feb 15 – Feb 22, 2026
Role: Writing and Redesign
Team: Raabit Hasan
Tools: Figma, Microsoft Paint
Heuristic Evaluation UI Redesign Wireframing Interaction Design Human-Centered Design

Context & Problem Statement

This project focused on analyzing and improving the usability of two websites: the Zara website and the Toronto Cupcake website. While both platforms serve different purposes, they share common usability issues that negatively impact user interaction. The primary problem was that several interface elements violated key interaction design principles such as visibility, consistency, feedback, constraints, and mapping. These issues made navigation inefficient, increased user effort, and created confusion during task completion. The goal of this project was to identify these issues and redesign the interfaces to improve usability and overall user experience.

Design Process

The design process followed a structured evaluation and redesign approach. First, we conducted a heuristic evaluation of both websites using established interaction design principles from HCI literature. We carefully examined how users interact with navigation menus, product displays, and interactive elements. Next, we identified specific usability issues under different principles:

  • Visibility: Important controls and navigation elements were not clearly distinguishable or easily noticeable.
  • Consistency: Layouts and interaction patterns varied across pages, making it harder for users to predict behavior.
  • Feedback: User actions such as clicking or selecting items often lacked immediate and clear system responses.
  • Constraints: The interfaces did not effectively guide users toward valid actions, leading to potential confusion.
  • Signifiers / Affordances: Some clickable elements were not visually distinguishable from non-interactive content.
  • Mapping: The relationship between controls and their outcomes was not always intuitive.
  • Fitts' Law: In the industrial website, the sidebar contained many small, tightly packed buttons placed far from the user’s starting cursor position, increasing interaction time and error probability.

Redesigns were created in Figma and Microsoft Paint by increasing button sizes, improving spacing, simplifying navigation, enhancing visual hierarchy, and adding clearer feedback mechanisms.

Final Outcome

The redesigned interfaces significantly improved usability by aligning with core interaction design principles. For example, in the sidebar redesign, multiple small buttons were replaced with larger, grouped categories. This reduced the precision required for interaction and made navigation faster and more intuitive. Similarly, improved visual hierarchy and spacing helped users quickly locate important elements, addressing visibility and attention issues. Across both websites, the redesigns resulted in:

  • Important controls and navigation elements were not clearly distinguishable or easily noticeable.
  • Layouts and interaction patterns varied across pages, making it harder for users to predict behavior.
  • User actions such as clicking or selecting items often lacked immediate and clear system responses.
  • The interfaces did not effectively guide users toward valid actions, leading to potential confusion.

Overall, the redesigned interfaces provide a more efficient and user-friendly experience compared to the original designs.

Reflection

This assignment helped me understand how small design decisions, such as button size or spacing, can have a major impact on usability. Applying interaction design principles in a real-world context made it clear that poor design is often not about missing features, but about how those features are presented and accessed. I also learned the importance of connecting theory to practice. Concepts like Fitts’ Law and visibility are not just abstract ideas but directly influence how users interact with interfaces. This project strengthened my ability to critically evaluate designs and propose meaningful improvements.
